# Referee Report Guidelines

Use this structure for the final output.

Write the referee report in LaTeX. Follow the installed `latex` skill when preparing the `.tex` output. Use section headings and numbered or bulleted lists that compile cleanly in standard LaTeX. Do not output plain prose or markdown as the final deliverable when the user asks for the report itself.

Before compiling, check for `\input{tcilatex}` and comment it out unless the user explicitly requires Scientific WorkPlace compatibility.

## Reviewer Perspective

Let the report reflect the user's actual expertise.

The report should feel strongest on:

- Quantitative dynamic macro.
- Structural labor economics.
- Heterogeneous-agent models in partial and general equilibrium.
- Financial frictions.
- Fiscal policy.
- Search and matching models.
- Labor market policy.
- Applied micro methods at the level of identification, implementation, and credible use in practice.

Be more cautious on specialized econometric theory or other areas outside that comparative advantage. In such cases, comment on identification, implementation, interpretation, and economic logic rather than pretending deeper technical expertise than the user has.

## 1. Overview and Overall Assessment

Write one short section, usually one to three paragraphs total.

Use an academic tone that matches the user's writing style. Keep the prose direct, fairly dry, and not overly polished. Use American English.

Include all of the following:

- The paper's central question and contribution.
- The main innovation relative to the existing literature, assessed using both the paper's own literature review and an independent literature review.
- Whether the paper is well positioned relative to the closest and most relevant papers in the field, but discuss this only briefly unless it is central to the paper's contribution claim.
- How the paper compares with the level of papers recently published in the submission journal.
- The main weaknesses or reasons the paper does not yet fully persuade at that journal's standard.
- A short overall assessment that stays measured and relatively positive in tone.
- A recommendation using one of these labels only: `Revise and resubmit`, `Accept with minor revision`, or `Rejection`.

Keep the assessment concise. Even for a likely rejection, prefer neutral and professional phrasing over harsh language.

Do not make the prose sound like polished editorial copy. It should read like strong academic writing by a fluent non-native speaker: clear, natural, and controlled, with American usage and without unnecessary stylistic flourishes.

`Revise and resubmit` and `Rejection` should be the most common outcomes. Use `Accept with minor revision` rarely, only when the paper is very close to publishable and the remaining changes are limited.

## 2. Major Comments

Write the number of major comments that fits the recommendation and the paper's complexity.

- For `Revise and resubmit`, write more major comments when necessary, up to `10` for especially complex papers.
- For `Accept with minor revision` or `Rejection`, write fewer major comments and concentrate on the most important issues.

Each major comment should have a short heading, usually a few words.

Do not force the comments into a preset breakdown. Let the set of major comments depend on the paper being refereed and on the issues that most affect its publishability, credibility, or fit with the submission journal's standard.

Develop each major comment in some depth. A major comment should usually do all of the following:

1. State the concern clearly.
2. Explain why it matters for the paper's argument, identification, model, interpretation, or contribution.
3. Clarify what makes it first-order rather than minor.
4. Give at least one concrete suggestion for how the authors could address it.

A major comment may also contain multiple parts or subpoints when that helps organize the argument. Make clear which parts are first-order and which are secondary.

Let the comments reflect the user's strongest areas of expertise when relevant, especially in dynamic models, structural labor, heterogeneous-agent settings, search and matching, fiscal policy, labor-market policy, and identification-based applied work.

Be more restrained when a paper's key contribution turns on technical areas outside that expertise. In those cases, avoid pretending full authority and focus instead on economic logic, identification, implementation, interpretation, and whether the claims are appropriately scoped.

Do not use fit with the literature as a default major comment category. Raise it only if literature omissions or mispositioning materially affect the contribution claim or the evaluation of novelty.

When it materially improves clarity, include a short equation, table, or figure in a major comment. These can come from the paper being refereed or from other papers in the literature, provided they are directly relevant to the point being made.

Do not complain in a generic way that references are outdated or incomplete. Raise a reference issue only when there is an important missing paper, and then name the paper and explain why it matters for the paper's contribution, comparison set, or interpretation.

Common critical comment patterns to consider only when relevant:

- Theoretical assumptions: ask whether a key assumption is plausible, whether it drives the main results, and whether the paper should provide a robustness check or an external-validity argument.
- Identification: ask whether the identification strategy is clear and correct, and whether the tests or supporting evidence used to validate identification are appropriate.
- Alternative mechanisms or algorithms: ask whether a result could be driven by a different mechanism, algorithm, or procedure than the one emphasized in the paper, and how the authors address those alternatives.

Useful suggestions include:

- Add a specific missing reference when it materially changes the comparison set or the interpretation of the contribution.
- Reframe the claimed contribution relative to the actual frontier.
- Clarify what is genuinely new versus incremental.
- Explain more clearly why the paper is a fit for the submission journal, or narrow the claims to fit that outlet better.
- Add a clarification, derivation, or intuition.
- Run a robustness or placebo exercise.
- Compare against a more relevant benchmark.
- Tighten the scope of the claim.
- Rework the identification argument.
- Add discussion of limitations.
- Revise the presentation of a table, figure, or appendix.

## 3. Minor Comments

Use short bullets for presentation issues that are easy to fix.

Minor comments do not require headings.

Do not include comments on data statements.

Keep minor comments distinct from major comments. Do not repeat, restate, or break out a major issue again in the minor-comments section.

For typos or copy-editing, mention only errors that materially affect the meaning of key sections, equations, results, or interpretation. Do not list trivial typos, tiny wording issues, small reference-format errors, or encoding problems that may reflect PDF conversion rather than the original file.

Typical minor comments:

- Equation typos or unclear notation that affect interpretation.
- Table labels, missing units, or formatting issues that impair readability.
- Figure readability and caption problems that affect interpretation.
- Important wording issues that can change the meaning of a key claim.

Do not move substantive concerns into this section.
## 4. References

Include a references section if the report cites any papers.

List all papers cited in the report in a consistent bibliography style. If no papers are cited, omit this section.
